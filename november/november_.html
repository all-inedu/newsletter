UC SCHOOLS: CALIFORNIA’S ACADEMIC POWERHOUSE

Whatever you’re planning to study, UC schools have it all. With 9 UC campuses offering undergraduate programs, choosing a UC school that is right for you can be challenging. From location to popular majors and acceptance rates, find a UC school that complements your goals.

With the application deadline coming, here’s your last chance of becoming a UC student. In terms of admissions, UC schools set themselves apart from other US institutions. Be it an all-in-one application or standardized-test blind approach, here are some things you should know before applying to any UC schools.

The UC application deadline is coming to an end this month, specifically on November 30th. How’s your application going? Have you finished your essays?

Worry not! Our mentor, Paul, who is a UC alumni, is sharing some tips on acing your UC applications. Find out how to leave a lasting impressions to the admissions officers:

  • Acing the Activities/Awards section

    While there are 20 activities you can fill out in this section, it is recommended to fill them out in the order of importance and theme:

    • Importance

      At the top are the ones that you have dedicated much of your time, energy, or effort into. You can also list the activities that are related to the major you’re pursuing or in line with your career interest.

    • Theme

      When it comes to the theme, group them together! For instance, if you are involved in a lot of science-related activities, you may group them all together to create a unique flow.

      As for awards, it shouldn't end at being in the academic honor roll or graduating as the school's valedictorian. You can fill out the awards that are related to your interests as well, whether it’s the arts, music, martial arts, or cheerleading

  • Wrapping everything up in the essays

    First off, do not start your essay by immediately and directly answering the essay prompts. If the prompt is ‘Describe the most significant challenge you faced’:

    • Don’t: Starting your essay with ‘the most significant challenge in my life is’

    • Do: Start with an anecdote that demonstrates the problem or experience without actually stating it

    Here’s a guide you can use when writing your essay:

    1. What you did

    2. The problems or challenges you faced

    3. How you solved or overcome these problems

    4. The impacts it had on you and your community

    5. The lessons you learned

    6. How you applied what you learned to other areas in your life

Success Story

Even when he was still in high school, Darren had set his eyes on political science and social issues. Aside from being actively involved in multiple extracurricular activities, including Model United Nations and the Student Council, he manages to fit in several work experiences.

While his impressive dedication to political science and social issues sounds serious, he’s a fun person who loves playing drums, swimming, and joking around with his peers.

His attitude of seizing every available opportunity shined during the university application period, where he aims for a scholarship in the US. All of his efforts paid off when he was awarded the Regents’ and Chancellor’s Scholarship and Koshland May Scholarship for UC Berkeley. With these scholarships—and a lot of hard work on his part—Darren is able to study at UC Berkeley with full scholarship!
